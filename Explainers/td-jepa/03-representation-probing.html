Exploration 03

Representation Probing

What does TD-JEPA actually learn? Linear probes reveal which state variables are encoded in the representation, and how they compare across methods.

What Linear Probes Reveal

A linear probe is a simple test: freeze the learned representation and train a linear classifier or regressor on top of it to predict some ground-truth quantity (position, velocity, heading). If the probe achieves high accuracy, the representation must contain that information in a linearly accessible form.

The heatmap below shows how strongly each representation dimension responds to different state variables. Hover over any cell to see the activation value and what it implies about the representation’s encoding.

Under TD-JEPA, you will notice a characteristic pattern: the heatmap shows structured blocks where groups of dimensions jointly encode related variables. Local features like position and velocity are encoded compactly, while value-relevant features like reachability and goal distance activate broader, more distributed patterns.

Standard JEPA shows strong encoding of local features but weak encoding of long-horizon variables. Random initialization shows only noise.

This structured encoding is predicted by the theoretical result that TD-JEPA learns a low-rank factorization of the policy-conditioned transition dynamics. Each “block” in the heatmap corresponds to a factor.

Probe Accuracy Across Methods

The plot below compares linear probe accuracy for several state variables across TD-JEPA and baseline methods. Select a variable with the buttons above the chart to see how each method fares.

The pattern is clear: all methods encode local proprioceptive variables well (position, velocity, heading achieve R-squared above 0.8). The divergence appears for global value-aware variables: TD-JEPA achieves R-squared of 0.85+ on reachability and value estimation, while standard JEPA drops below 0.5 and methods like ICVF and Laplacian fall further behind.

Proprioceptive vs Visual Observations

TD-JEPA accepts either proprioceptive state vectors or raw pixel images. The representation learned from pixels must implicitly recover the relevant state variables through the encoder. The visualization below shows how probe accuracy differs between the two observation modes.

Toggle between modes to see the gap. Notably, TD-JEPA’s gap between proprioception and vision is smaller than other methods, suggesting its TD-based objective helps the encoder extract value-relevant features even from high-dimensional pixel inputs.

The results from the paper corroborate this visualization: TD-JEPA achieves 628.8 on DMC RGB (versus 661.2 on proprioception), a gap of only 32.4 points. Compare this to FB (648.2 proprio vs 456.2 RGB, gap of 192) or HILP (620.1 vs 391.2, gap of 229). The TD objective acts as a form of implicit curriculum that guides the visual encoder toward task-relevant features.

On OGBench, the proprioceptive advantage is less pronounced: TD-JEPA scores 41.34% (RGB) vs 37.98% (proprio). This suggests that for some tasks, the visual representation may even capture additional contextual cues that proprioception misses.

This hierarchical organization is a natural consequence of the low-rank factorization: the most significant singular vectors capture the coarsest structure, with each subsequent dimension adding refinement.
