Exploration 01

Temporal Difference
Meets JEPA

Standard JEPA predicts the next latent state. TD-JEPA replaces this with a temporal difference target that captures long-horizon dynamics — the key innovation that enables zero-shot RL.

The Problem with One-Step Prediction

A standard Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) encodes an observation into a latent vector and then trains a predictor to produce the next latent state. This one-step regression loss captures local transition dynamics beautifully, but it is fundamentally myopic.

Consider an agent at a fork in a maze: one path leads to a dead end in three steps, the other to a reward in twenty. A one-step predictor sees both paths as equally promising — the next state is just one step away in either direction. Reinforcement learning, however, needs representations that encode the long-term consequences of choosing one path over another.

In standard JEPA, the predictor f learns to map the current latent state to the next latent state. The loss is a simple regression:

LJEPA = || f(φ(s)) − φ(s′) ||2

TD-JEPA replaces the target φ(s') with a temporal-difference bootstrap. The predictor now regresses against the task encoding of the next state plus a discounted version of its own future prediction, stopped from propagating gradients:

LTD = || fk(φ(s)) − (ψ(s′) + γ · sg(fk(φ(s′)))) ||2

This seemingly small change has a profound effect. Unrolling the recursion reveals that the predictor is learning successor features in latent space: a discounted sum of all future task encodings under policy k. The representation now captures not just what is adjacent, but what lies far ahead.

The discount factor γ controls the horizon. At γ = 0 the TD target collapses to the next-step encoding (equivalent to standard JEPA). As γ approaches 1, the representation captures increasingly long-horizon dynamics.

What the Representation Captures

A concrete way to see the difference: consider two states that look identical locally (same immediate surroundings) but lead to very different outcomes. Standard JEPA would assign them nearly identical representations, because the next state is the same. TD-JEPA pushes their representations apart, because the long-run consequences diverge.

The interactive below shows a simplified 8-dimensional representation space. Each bar represents one dimension of the learned feature vector. Toggle between JEPA and TD-JEPA to see how the representation changes for a pair of states that are locally similar but have different long-term value.

The theoretical result underlying this figure is that an idealized TD-JEPA learns encoders that capture a low-rank factorization of the long-term policy-conditioned transition dynamics. The predictor, in turn, approximates successor features — the expected discounted sum of future encodings under a given policy. This is precisely the information needed for zero-shot policy selection given a new reward function.

The Role of the Discount Factor

The discount factor γ is the single most important hyperparameter in TD-JEPA. It controls how far into the future the representation looks. Drag the slider below to see how representations change as the horizon grows.

γ 0.00

At γ = 0, the TD target reduces to ψ(s') alone, and the method degenerates to standard JEPA. As γ grows, each additional 0.01 expands the effective horizon by roughly 1/(1-γ) steps. At γ = 0.99, the representation looks roughly 100 steps ahead  — enough for most continuous control tasks.

In practice, Bagatella et al. use γ = 0.99 for all experiments. This matches the standard discount factor in RL and ensures the representation captures the full task-relevant horizon.
