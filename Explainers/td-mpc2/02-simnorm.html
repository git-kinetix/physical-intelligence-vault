Exploration 02

SimNorm: Simplicial
Normalization

A key innovation of TD-MPC2 is SimNorm — projecting latent representations onto simplices via softmax, biasing toward sparsity without the hard constraints of VQ-VAE. This prevents representation collapse and stabilizes training.

In standard continuous latent spaces, representations can collapse: all inputs map to nearly the same point, destroying useful information. This is a common failure mode for model-based RL, where the world model must maintain diverse, information-rich embeddings across different states.

SimNorm addresses this by partitioning the latent vector into L groups of dimension V, then applying softmax independently to each group. Each group is thereby projected onto a V-dimensional simplex — a generalization of the triangle to arbitrary dimensions.

SimNorm(z) = [softmax(z1 / τ), ..., softmax(zL / τ)]    τ = temperature
In the paper, the latent state is partitioned into L = 8 groups each of dimension V = 16 for the 5M base model. Each group sums to 1 after SimNorm, living on a 15-dimensional simplex.

The Simplex Geometry

A simplex is the simplest polytope in any dimension: a line segment in 1D, an equilateral triangle in 2D, a tetrahedron in 3D. Each point on a V-dimensional simplex represents a probability distribution over V outcomes. The vertices correspond to one-hot vectors (complete certainty), while the center is the uniform distribution (maximum uncertainty).

LayerNorm vs SimNorm

TD-MPC2 uses both LayerNorm and SimNorm in its encoder: LayerNorm normalizes each layer's activations, while SimNorm is applied to the final latent representation. The critical difference is that SimNorm constrains outputs to the simplex, which provides a structural bias toward sparsity and diversity.

Why SimNorm Prevents Collapse

Representation collapse occurs when the encoder maps all inputs to nearly identical latent vectors — the world model then cannot distinguish between states. The ablation results from the paper are striking: removing normalization entirely drops performance from 54.2 to ~35 on the 80-task benchmark.

SimNorm's simplex constraint provides a geometric guarantee: since each group sums to 1, the representation cannot collapse to a single point. Different inputs will be forced to distribute their "probability mass" differently across dimensions, maintaining diversity.
