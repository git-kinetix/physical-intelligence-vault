Exploration 01

Model Predictive Control
via MPPI Planning

TD-MPC2 plans by sampling action sequences, rolling them out through a learned world model, and iteratively refining toward high-reward trajectories. This is Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control.

Unlike model-free RL agents that act from a fixed policy, TD-MPC2 plans at inference time. Given the current state, the planner imagines many possible futures by rolling action sequences through its learned latent dynamics model. It then selects the action sequence most likely to maximize cumulative reward.

This approach is called Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control. The key parameters are the number of sampled trajectories (512 in the paper), the planning horizon (H = 3 steps), and the temperature that controls how aggressively the planner concentrates on the best trajectories.

The policy prior guides initial sampling, reducing the number of samples needed. Without it, MPPI would require far more trajectories to find good actions from scratch.

Iterative Refinement

MPPI does not simply pick the best sample. Instead, it refines the sampling distribution across multiple iterations. After evaluating all trajectories, it computes a weighted mean (with temperature-controlled softmax weighting), then resamples around this new mean. Watch the trajectories converge below.

The Role of the Policy Prior

In TD-MPC2, the planner does not sample from a uniform distribution. Instead, initial action samples are drawn from the learned policy prior — a stochastic policy trained to approximate the planner's output. This dramatically improves sample efficiency: the policy provides a warm start, and MPPI only needs to refine locally.

The ablation results from the paper confirm this synergy: planning alone scores ~53.7 on the 80-task benchmark, policy alone ~48, but planning + policy together reach ~54.2. The policy prior also enables fast action selection when compute is limited.

At test time, TD-MPC2 executes only the first action of the best trajectory, then replans from the next observed state. This is the "receding horizon" principle of model predictive control.
