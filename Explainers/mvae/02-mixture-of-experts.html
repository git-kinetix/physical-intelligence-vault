Exploration 02

Mixture of Experts Decoder

A single feedforward decoder struggles to cover the full diversity of human motion. MVAE uses a Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture with 6 expert networks, each specializing on different motion patterns.

Why does a single decoder network fail? Human motion is multi-modal: walking, running, turning, stopping, and idle standing have fundamentally different dynamics. A single network must average over these modes, producing blurry, artifact-laden motion. The MoE decoder solves this by routing different inputs to different specialized experts, blended by a learned gating network.

pt = Σi=16 gi(pt-1, z) · Ei(pt-1, z)

The gating network g takes the previous pose and latent code as input and outputs a softmax distribution over the 6 experts. Each expert Ei independently predicts the next pose, and the final output is their weighted blend. A critical design choice is that z is injected at every layer of each expert network, not just the input layer. This prevents posterior collapse — without it, the decoder learns to ignore z entirely and just copies the input pose.

Both the gating network and each expert are three-layer feedforward networks with 256 hidden units and ELU activations. The gating network's output passes through a softmax to produce blending weights that sum to 1.

Ablated
Toggle expert buttons to disable them. Removing key experts introduces visible artifacts: foot skating, jerky transitions, and loss of motion diversity. The paper found that reducing from 6 to 4 experts consistently increased foot skating.

The results from the paper confirm what this ablation demonstrates: reducing from 6 to 4 experts increases foot skating from 0.067 to 0.085 cm/frame on random walks, and from 0.24 to 0.38 cm/frame on maze navigation. The 6-expert configuration also produces more symmetric turning behavior (1.70 s left vs. 1.56 s right), compared to the 4-expert model's pronounced handedness bias (1.63 s left vs. 1.11 s right).

Latent Injection Depth

One of the paper's key architectural insights is that z must be passed to every layer of each expert, not just the input. The figure below illustrates why. When z is only at the input, the deep layers learn to ignore it, and the model collapses to a deterministic copy of the previous pose (posterior collapse). When z penetrates every layer, the decoder remains dependent on the latent code, preserving the VAE's generative capability.

Compare how injection depth affects the decoder's use of the latent code. "Input Only" shows partial collapse; "No z" shows full posterior collapse where all z samples produce identical output.
Posterior collapse is a well-known failure mode of VAEs where the decoder learns to ignore the latent code, making the KL term trivially zero. The beta=0.2 weighting further mitigates this by under-penalizing the KL divergence.

Expert Count: 4 vs. 6

The paper ablates the number of experts (N=4 vs. N=6) and finds consistent improvements from having more experts. The visualization below compares the two configurations side by side across the paper's key metrics. With 4 experts, foot skating nearly doubles on maze navigation (0.24 to 0.38 cm/frame), and a pronounced handedness bias emerges: the 4-expert model turns right in 1.11 seconds but takes 1.63 seconds to turn left.

This asymmetry is attributed to the interaction between RL exploration and a limited expert pool. With fewer experts, the RL agent discovers effective right-turning strategies early and exploits them, leading to an asymmetric policy. With 6 experts, there is enough capacity for symmetric specialization, and the model achieves 1.70 s left vs. 1.56 s right — a much more balanced controller.

Comparison of N=4 and N=6 expert configurations. The 6-expert model achieves lower foot skating, more symmetric turning, and comparable response times. Data from Tables 1 and 2 of the paper.

The soft specialization of the MoE decoder is one of MVAE's most elegant contributions. Rather than requiring explicit motion labels to assign motions to different networks (as in earlier phase-based methods), the gating network discovers the natural modes of human motion during training. The 6-expert configuration provides enough capacity to cover the full diversity of the motion capture data while maintaining smooth blending between motion types.

This insight has influenced subsequent work in character animation, where mixture-of-experts architectures have become a standard component for handling multi-modal motion distributions. The key lesson is that soft routing outperforms hard assignment — real human motion exists on a continuum, and the gating network's continuous blending weights capture this better than discrete motion categories ever could.
