Exploration 03

Scheduled Sampling & Error Drift

Training an autoregressive model is delicate. Teacher forcing gives perfect inputs during training but leads to compounding errors at test time. MVAE uses a scheduled sampling curriculum to bridge this gap.

The fundamental challenge of autoregressive generation is exposure bias. During teacher-forced training, the decoder always receives ground-truth previous poses as input. But at test time, it receives its own predictions — which contain small errors. These errors compound over time, causing the generated motion to drift away from the natural motion manifold. After a few seconds, the character may be skating, floating, or contorting into impossible poses.

Input at time t = pt-1GT with probability (1 - s),   p̂t-1 with probability s

MVAE's solution is a three-phase training curriculum spanning 180 epochs. The sampling probability s starts at 0 (pure teacher forcing) and gradually increases to 1 (pure autoregressive), with a critical transition period in between. This gives the model time to first learn the basic motion dynamics, then adapt to its own prediction errors.

At a rollout of 8 frames (the training horizon), both models look reasonable. But extend to 30+ frames and the difference becomes stark. The teacher-forced model's errors compound exponentially: each prediction's small error shifts the input distribution further from what the model saw during training. By 60 frames, the character's feet slide across the ground and the limbs begin to stretch. By 120 frames, the motion has collapsed entirely.

In contrast, the scheduled-sampling model has learned to correct for its own errors. During phase 2 of training, it was gradually exposed to increasingly noisy inputs (its own predictions). This acts as a form of self-supervised error correction — the model learns not just to predict the next pose, but to do so robustly even when the input is imperfect.

The paper's training schedule uses 20 epochs of pure teacher forcing to bootstrap the basic dynamics, 20 epochs of linear ramp from s=0 to s=1, and then 140 epochs of pure autoregressive training to fully adapt. This asymmetric schedule — heavily weighted toward autoregressive training — reflects the fact that the model will always run autoregressively at test time.

The total training time is approximately 2 hours on a GTX 1060, a remarkably modest computational requirement. The Adam optimizer's learning rate is linearly decayed from 1e-4 to zero, and the mini-batch size is 64. These choices, combined with the scheduled sampling curriculum, produce a generative model that can run for thousands of frames without visible degradation.

The training data consists of only 17 minutes of motion capture from the CMU database (~30,000 frames including mirrored augmentation). No motion labels, foot contact annotations, or gait-phase information are required.

Training Hyperparameters

The scheduled sampling curriculum is only one piece of the training recipe. Several other design choices work together to ensure stable, high-quality motion generation. The visualization below shows how the key hyperparameters interact during training: learning rate decay, beta weighting, and rollout length.

beta=0.20
The interplay of training hyperparameters. Learning rate decays linearly from 1e-4 to 0 over 180 epochs. Beta=0.2 balances reconstruction accuracy with latent space regularity. Higher beta forces a smoother latent space but reduces motion quality; lower beta risks posterior collapse.

The choice of β = 0.2 is particularly important. In a standard VAE, β = 1 weighs reconstruction and KL divergence equally, but this often leads to posterior collapse in sequential models. The reduced weight gives the reconstruction term more influence, ensuring the decoder needs the latent code to produce accurate next-pose predictions. But β cannot be too low either — some KL pressure is needed to maintain a smooth, well-organized latent space that RL can navigate effectively.

The paper validates this with a comparison: β = 0.4 reduces foot skating on some tasks (target reaching: 0.15 vs. 0.27 cm/frame) but increases it on others (joystick: 0.33 vs. 0.28 cm/frame). There is no universally "best" beta; the default of 0.2 represents a robust compromise across all tasks and conditions.

Why It Matters

Scheduled sampling is not unique to MVAE — it was first proposed by Bengio et al. (2015) for sequence-to-sequence models in NLP. But its application to motion generation reveals a deeper principle: autoregressive models must be trained in the conditions under which they will be deployed. For character animation, this means training with the model's own (imperfect) predictions as input.

Without this curriculum, the MVAE would produce beautiful single-step predictions but fail catastrophically at the multi-second rollouts required for real-time character control. The scheduled sampling curriculum is what transforms a next-frame predictor into a stable long-horizon motion generator — the foundation on which all four RL control tasks are built.

The entire MVAE training pipeline — from raw motion capture data to a controller navigating procedural mazes — can be completed in under 8 hours on a single desktop GPU (2 hours for the MVAE + 6 hours for the most complex RL task). This efficiency makes the approach practical for real game development pipelines, a key consideration for the Electronic Arts researchers who developed it.
