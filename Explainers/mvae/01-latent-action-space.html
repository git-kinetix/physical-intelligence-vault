Exploration 01

The Latent Action Space

In MVAE, the encoder compresses pairs of consecutive poses into a 32-dimensional latent code z. This code is not just a compressed representation — it serves as the action that a reinforcement learning agent selects to drive the character.

Traditional character controllers operate in high-dimensional joint-angle or torque spaces. MVAE replaces this with a much more compact and structured alternative: the latent space of a trained variational autoencoder. The encoder takes two consecutive poses (pt-1 and pt) and produces a mean and variance for a 32-dimensional Gaussian. Through the reparameterization trick, we sample a latent code z that captures the essential "intent" of the motion transition.

q(z | pt-1, pt) = N(μ, σ2)    z ∈ R32

Notice how the latent space organizes itself into coherent regions without any motion labels. Walking motions cluster near the center, running occupies a ring at higher energy, and transitional motions (turning, stopping) fill the spaces between. This emergent structure is what makes the latent space viable as an action space for RL — nearby codes produce similar motions, giving the policy a smooth optimization landscape.

The encoder uses three fully connected layers with 256 hidden units and ELU activations. The latent dimension of 32 was chosen to balance expressiveness with compactness — the RL agent needs to search this space efficiently.

The β = 0.2 weighting in the KL divergence term is crucial here. A higher β would force the latent space to be more Gaussian-like (smoother interpolation, but less expressive). A lower β would allow sharper mode separation at the cost of possible "dead zones" where sampling produces artifacts. The MVAE authors found 0.2 to be the sweet spot for maintaining both smooth coverage and motion quality.

Sampling vs. Policy Selection

The figure below contrasts two ways of selecting latent codes. On the left, random sampling from the prior p(z) = N(0, I) produces varied but undirected motion — the character wanders aimlessly. On the right, a trained PPO policy selects codes strategically, producing goal-directed movement. The policy output is clipped to [-4, +4] via Tanh scaling, keeping the agent within the high-density region of the latent space.

The difference is striking. Random sampling produces plausible motion at each frame — the VAE's learned prior ensures that — but without coherent direction. The PPO policy, trained with task rewards in the latent space, produces purposeful locomotion. This demonstrates the key insight of MVAE: the VAE handles motion quality while RL handles task completion, a clean separation of concerns.

The PPO controller uses two hidden layers of 256 units with ReLU activations. Training uses 100 parallel simulations and takes 1-6 hours per task on a desktop GPU.

The Encoder-Decoder Pipeline

To understand why the latent space works as an action space, it helps to trace the full encoder-decoder pipeline. The encoder takes a pair of consecutive poses and compresses them into a 32-dimensional Gaussian. The decoder takes the previous pose plus a latent sample and predicts the next pose. This asymmetry is key: the latent code captures the transition dynamics between poses, not the pose itself.

The visualization below shows the pipeline in action. The left column shows the encoder processing two input frames. The center shows the bottleneck where the latent code is sampled. The right column shows the decoder producing the output pose. The flowing particles represent information being compressed and then expanded.

The encoder-decoder pipeline. Two consecutive poses enter the encoder (left), are compressed to a 32-dim latent code z (center), and decoded into the next pose prediction (right). Each encoder/decoder has three 256-unit layers with ELU activations.

A crucial detail is the pose representation used by MVAE. Each pose includes root position, root facing direction, linear velocities, joint positions in 3D, joint velocities in 3D, and joint orientations encoded as 6D forward/upward vectors. This rich representation gives the model enough information to predict physically plausible transitions without needing explicit foot contact labels or gait phase annotations.

The choice of 6D rotation representation for joint orientations is particularly important. Unlike Euler angles (which suffer from gimbal lock) or quaternions (which have antipodal ambiguity), the 6D representation provides a continuous, singularity-free parameterization that makes regression loss well-behaved during training.

Four Control Tasks

The MVAE framework is evaluated on four diverse control tasks, all operating through the same 32-dimensional latent action space. An energy penalty E = v_root^2 + (1/J) * sum(|v_joints|) is applied across all tasks to discourage excessive, unnatural motion. The tasks range from simple point-to-point navigation to complex maze exploration with limited perception.

Four control tasks evaluated in the paper. Target reaching: navigate to random points in a 120x80ft arena. Joystick: follow directional commands at varying speeds. Path following: track a figure-8 path with 4 lookahead targets. Maze: explore a procedural maze using 16-ray vision sensing.

What makes these results remarkable is that a single MVAE model trained on just 17 minutes of motion capture data supports all four tasks. The RL controllers are task-specific, but they all operate in the same shared latent space. This demonstrates the generality of the learned motion prior: it captures enough about human locomotion to support any downstream control objective, from simple waypoint navigation to complex spatial reasoning.
