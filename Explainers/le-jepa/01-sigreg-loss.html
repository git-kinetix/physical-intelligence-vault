The SIGReg Loss Landscape

Visualizing the Sketched Isotropic Gaussian Regularization that keeps Le-JEPA from collapsing — and why it works where VICReg and Barlow Twins struggle.

Why Regularize?

Every self-supervised method faces the same nemesis: representation collapse. Without supervision, a network can minimize its loss by simply mapping every input to the same constant vector — a degenerate solution that is useless for downstream tasks.

Prior methods fight collapse with increasingly complex heuristics: stop-gradient (BYOL), EMA teacher-student (DINO, I-JEPA), covariance regularization (VICReg), cross-correlation constraints (Barlow Twins). Le-JEPA replaces all of these with a single, principled regularizer: SIGReg.

L = Lpred + λ · LSIGReg Only one hyperparameter: λ

The Characteristic Function Test

SIGReg enforces that the embedding distribution matches an isotropic Gaussian N(0, I) by comparing their characteristic functions. The characteristic function of a distribution is its Fourier transform — if two distributions have the same characteristic function, they are identical.

LSIGReg = Σk | φ̂(ωk) − exp(−||ωk||²/2) |² Empirical char. func. vs. Gaussian char. func. at random frequencies ωk

Collapse vs. Diversity Trade-off

The λ hyperparameter controls the balance between prediction quality and embedding diversity. Too low and the model collapses; too high and it ignores the prediction objective altogether. Le-JEPA's key finding is that a single fixed λ works across 60+ architectures — no scheduling or tuning needed.

Comparison: SIGReg vs. VICReg vs. Barlow Twins

To see why SIGReg matters, compare the loss landscapes of three regularization approaches. VICReg penalizes variance/covariance/invariance with separate terms (3 hyperparameters). Barlow Twins constrains the cross-correlation matrix. SIGReg uses a single characteristic function match. The key advantage: SIGReg has linear complexity O(BK) while both VICReg and Barlow Twins require the full covariance matrix at O(Bd²).

Linear Complexity

A crucial practical advantage of SIGReg is its computational cost. Computing the full covariance matrix of a d-dimensional embedding across B samples costs O(Bd²), which becomes prohibitive for large embedding dimensions. SIGReg instead evaluates the characteristic function at K random frequency slices, costing only O(BKd) — linear in all dimensions.
