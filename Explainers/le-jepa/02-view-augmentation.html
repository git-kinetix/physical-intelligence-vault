Multi-View Augmentation

How Le-JEPA creates multiple views of an image to learn invariant representations — and why it does not need a teacher-student (EMA) framework.

Views Are the Supervision

In self-supervised learning, the training signal comes from the data itself. Le-JEPA creates multiple augmented views of each image and trains the encoder to produce consistent representations across views. If two crops of the same dog produce similar embeddings, the network has learned something meaningful about "dog-ness."

The Shared-Weight Architecture

A critical difference between Le-JEPA and its predecessors (I-JEPA, DINO) is the absence of separate "online" and "target" networks. In methods like DINO, a teacher network is maintained as an exponential moving average (EMA) of the student. Le-JEPA eliminates this entirely: both views are processed by the same encoder with shared weights.

zx = fθ(x)    zy = fθ(y)    Lpred = || gφ(zx) − zy ||² Same encoder f_θ for both views. No stop-gradient. No EMA.

Eliminating EMA reduces Le-JEPA's implementation to approximately 50 lines of code for the full distributed training loop. This simplicity is a direct consequence of having a theoretically grounded regularizer (SIGReg) instead of heuristic stability tricks.

View Configuration Matters

The number and type of views significantly impacts performance. Le-JEPA's ablations reveal a clear hierarchy: at least 2 global views are essential (jumping from 53% to 72% accuracy), while additional local views provide diminishing returns.

Augmentation Pairs Create the Prediction Task

The multi-view strategy generates pairs for the prediction loss. From each pair, one view is the context (input to the predictor) and the other is the target (what the predictor tries to reconstruct in embedding space). All pairs from the same image contribute to the loss.

4
Each view is paired with every other view. The predictor g learns to map z_context to z_target. More views = more training signal per image, but with diminishing returns.

Why No EMA Works

The EMA trick in prior methods serves as an implicit regularizer: the slowly-updating teacher prevents the student from changing too fast, which would cause collapse. But SIGReg provides a much stronger guarantee — it directly constrains the embedding distribution to be Gaussian, making the EMA crutch unnecessary.
